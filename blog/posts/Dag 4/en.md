Today marks three years since I lost my father to brain cancer. This post is quite personal and philosophical. Beware that it may contain a few clichés.

[photo:1]

Life is strange.

When losing someone very close to our hearts, it all feels surreal and awry, for a long time. In 2017 I was told by medical professionals that he had brain tumors. Years later we were told the tumors were malign and metastasizing. In April 2023 I sat by the hospice bed for several days — Held his hand, slowly said goodbye and grieved that his life soon would be over. Indeed, we knew all along his time with us was limited. But it never made it easier to accept and internalise the fact that he is gone for good.

## Lost in sight, but not in my mind

A couple weeks ago I dreamt that he was with us and everything was just like before the cancer diagnosis happened. As I woke up and realised it was all a dream I felt a seriously deep sorrow. I can't say I think about my father every day, but with so many memories and moments shared I am very often reminded of him. It still feels tremendously unfair that he got taken away by cancer, but I have no choice other than to come to terms and accept it as it is, no matter how hard it is.

He hadn't even turned 60 yet, and hadn't it been for this horrible cancer he would have been with us for quite many years I'm sure. He had worked his whole life, a strong and hardy man, just to contract a horrible disease. I feel wrecked thinking about it. There are many things I would want to share with him. My way of dealing with the grief and sorrow is to imagine my father is up there watching me like an angel.

[photo:2]

## A new perspective 

This whole cancer-carousel changed my perspectives. Firstly, the number one truth in life is that life is unfair. However, if you are reading this you have so many things to be grateful for. Gratefulness is the number one source to happiness. Even though I no longer have a father I am happy he got to stay alive for 6 years with brain tumors. I am I can't say I think about him every day, but with so many memories and moments shared I am very often reminded of him heartbroken to not have my father at Christmas, Graduation, or eventually my future marriage, but I remain very happy for all the time and memories we shared. These memories matter. I have learnt that creating memories is the most valuable thing we can do. What else matters when we eventually reach the point of no,

We should not take things for granted. Big or small. Be grateful! Try to enjoy what you have and seize opportunities to make life more fulfilling and enjoyable. Challenge yourself because it is actually a privilege to feel challenged. A bunch of these clichés rhyme well with the trip. I'm enjoying what life has to offer, basically.
