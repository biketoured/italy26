Today marks three years since I lost my father to brain cancer. This post is quite personal and philosophical, but I'd like to share some thoughts to those who are interested.

[photo:1]

Life is strange.
In 2017 my father was diagnosed with brain tumors. Years later we learned they were malignant and spreading. In April 2023 I sat by the hospice bed for several days. Held his hand, slowly said goodbye, and grieved that his life was almost over. We had known for a long time that his time with us was limited. It never made it easier.
## Lost in sight, but not in my mind
He hadn't even turned 60. If it hadn't been for the cancer he would have been with us for many years, a strong, hardy man who had worked his whole life, taken away from us too soon by a horrible disease. A couple of weeks ago I dreamt he was with us and everything was like before the diagnosis. When I woke up and realised it was just a dream I felt a deep sorrow. I'm wrecked thinking about everything I still want to share with him.
My way of carrying the grief is to imagine he's up there watching over me, even though I never believed in such things before he passed away...

## A new perspective
This whole experience changed my perspectives. Nothing in life is guaranteed, and we tend not to fully appreciate what we have until it's gone. Even so, I'm grateful he stayed with us for six years after the diagnosis. It's important to mourn, but life goes on, and I've had to accept that, however unfair and sad it feels.
I'm heartbroken to not have him at Christmas, at my graduation, or one day at my wedding. But I'm genuinely happy for every single memory we made together. Knowing his time was limited made me appreciate him more deeply. Maybe that's the thing about loss and death, it gets you to appreciate what you have while you still can.
