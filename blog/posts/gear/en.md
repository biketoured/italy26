# How do I prioritise when packing?
Packing for 40 days on a bike may sound a bit overwhelming. With just three bags strapped to the bike one needs to reconsider every single item.

### An economist's assignment...
As a nerdy economist, packing is one of my favourite parts of the trip since it is actually a challenging optimisation problem without a correct answer (just like most problems in economics the solution is often found in some kind of best-estimate equilibrium between costs and beenfits). There are multiple constraints; space, weight, usefulness, and budget. <br><br>
In economic theory, you often try to maximise the expected <em> utility </em>, satisfaction, of a decision while minimising the expected costs. Each item I pack has its own use-case that will bring me satisfaction (and also have me avoid dissatisfaction). The items I bring need to make me self-sufficient, comfortable, light, adaptive to things that can happen along the way. However, all items come with costs in terms of <bo>1) adding weight</bo>, <bo>2) monetary costs</bo>, and <bo>3) taking up space</bo> where I could have had other items. The implicit 'costs' of allocating money, weight or space on one item in stead of the best alternative is referred to as an 'opportunity cost'.<br><br>

Example: The opportunity cost of bringing a puffy jacket is fairly large, because it takes up much space where I could have fitted a set of clothing, say a long-sleeved shirt, a cap, a buff and some warm socks. If we assume both options make me warm, the set of clothes is more multifunctional than the jacket, and easier to adapt to the temperature. I will therefore not bring the jacket, but instead pick these versatile substitutes. Similarly, I opt for 'barefoot shoes' that are very versatile and take up much less space than any regular kind of shoes, saving me space and weight without compromising too much at any terms. It would be a hassle to bring valuable equipment I don't end up needing 4000km through Italy. I'm leaving the puffy jacket, jogging shoes, and favourite tea cup at home. They are all useful but not useful enough to justify their opportunity costs. I would likely regret bringing jogging shoes knowing that I could have brought band-aids and a pair of hiking pants instead. Opportunity cost depends on the next best item to bring, based on my evaluation.

# Packing list:
With the theory laid out, let's see if I live up to my ambitions...<br>
After the journey through Sweden I got some valuable insights on what to bring and what not to, here's what makes the cut for this adventure:

## Bike
Trek Checkpoint ALR 5, along with lights, a bikelock and a ringing bell that I will use to scare people, unintentionally :). Two water bottle holders and a tyre pump.

## Bags

Four bags in total: a frame bag, a handlebar bag, a saddle bag, and a small snack pouch (the snack bag is perfect for stashing focaccia or cannoli!). Yes, I will live for over a month on my bike and three bags. This is just an overly complicated way to simulate being homeless I guess :)

## Clothing

Two pairs of bibs, three jerseys, three sets of underwear, one light rain jacket. A packable bike poncho made specially for wearing over myself and the bike. In Italy in May there is no need for a winter kit, but I can layer-over-layer to get some pretty effective warming. Helmet and glasses to protect my head. I'll also bring some casual clothes so I can eat at restaurant with some dignity :).

## Tools & spares

Bicycle punp, tyre levers, patch kit, a multitool, zip ties, chain wax, and a spare link if the chain breaks. If something bigger breaks, I'll just find a bike shop. Italy has bike shops. Lots of them.

## Cooking & Sleeping

Tent with all its amendments, sleeping bag, inflatable mattress and pillow. I decided to not bring the cooking kit. Primariy because it has a high opportunity cost, it takes up a lot of space and weighs quite a bit along with a gas cannister. Additionally, eating out at restaurants is worth the extra cost, and I could always complement it with all kinds of produce from supermarkets and bakeries. Cooking kit was definitively useful through Sweden, but I expect it has less utility for this journey. There is also a third argument. Too many things to think about (where can I cook, what do I cook, where can I do the dishes, where can I find water...) may contribute to <em>decision fatigue</em>, taking up mindspace from what really matters. Since I love italian food I might aswell leave the cooking kit at home and opt for restaurants! Regardless, decision fatigue may persist... Everyone knows picking from a restaurant menu can be a seriously tough decision!

## Electronics and miscellaneous

Phone, watch, power bank, earphones, chargers. Passport, wallet and EU healthcate insurance card. Some first-aid kit stuff, a flashlight, knife and flint and steel just in case i need it. Hiking pants as a middle ground solution to semi-casual pants and warm pants. Sunscreen, after sun, hand moisturiser, toiletries (shaving products, EdT, soap, toothbrush...). A light microfiber towel, disinfection, light gloves, plastic bags, zip ties, plastic bags, a pencil, some spare parts. All the necessary stuff.

## Unusual items
Here are some more unusual gamechangers: <br>
Earplugs and blindfold: I feel in love with birdsong and sunlight, but not when trying to sleep. Earplugs and blindfold was a gamechanger in Sweden and Norway with the midnight sun. In fact, my blindfold is a textile reusable face mask. Multifunctional again :)<br>
Spork, and a small salt and pepper container: Regardless of whether I bring the cooking kit or not, a spork will come in handy. Salt to replenish the electrolytes and black pepper because it tastes amazing with anything.<br>
A compact package of high energy snacks: In case I get an energy dip or miscalculate the effort over a mountain pass it could be tremendously helpful to have an energy reserve.<br><br>
Putting my rational self away, I also give some room for items my creative, emotional self would like to bring regardless of the cost:<br>
A block of blank pages: Wherein I can write a diary, put memoirs, or draw paintings.<br>
12 colouring pens: I bring these in case I get the feeling to create a strong memory of some location by drawing it. I want to give room to my creative side in this adventure. I haven't drawn anything serious for years, but I feel an urge to try doing it, I'm curious on learning by doing.<br>
A harmonica(?): I have been thinking about buying one for a while, it feels like a perfect instrument for bikepacking. Playing some blue chords at the nightfall while washing off the impressions of a long day. Might be aquiring one on the fly. We'll see what the future brings! <br>
Earphones: Take up little space and could be nice to have when calling someone or as noice-supression when going through crowded cities. <br>

## The overarching rule

If it doesn't earn its weight and space, it doesn't come. Less is more.
