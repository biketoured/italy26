# What do I pack for this adventure?
Packing for 40 days on a bike may sound a bit overwhelming. With just three bags strapped to the bike one needs to reconsider every single item.

### An economist's assignment...
Packing is one of my favourite parts of the trip since it is actually a challenging optimisation problem without a correct answer. There are multiple constraints; space, weight, usefulness, and budget. <br><br>
In economic theory, we often try to maximise the expected <em> utility </em>, satisfaction, of a decision. This is done by maximising the expected utility, 'satisfaction', while minimising the expected costs, 'dissatisfaction'. Each item I pack has its own use-case that will bring me satisfaction (and also have me avoid dissatisfaction). However, they all come with costs in terms of <bo>1) adding weight</bo>, <bo>2) monetary costs</bo>, and <bo>3)taking up space</bo> where I could have had other items. The implicit 'cost' of having to lose out on other items is referred to as opportunity cost. The items I bring need to make me self-sufficient, comfortable, light, adaptive and prepared for most things that can happen along the way.<br><br>

Example: The opportunity cost of bringing a puffy jacket is fairly large, because it takes up much space where I could have fitted anything else, say a long-sleeved shirt, a cap, a buff and some warm socks. Both options make me warm but the set of clothes is more multifunctional than the jacket, and easier to adapt to the temperature. I will therefore not bring the jacket, but instead pick these versatile substitutes. Similarly, I opt for barefoot shoes that are very versatile and take up much less space than any regular kind of shoes, saving me space and weight without compromising too much at any terms. Even though I'm not racing it would be a hassle to bring valuable stuff I won't need 4000km through Italy. I'm leaving the puffy jacket, fine shoes, and favourite tea cup at home. Each excessive hectogram might slow me down by hours on the course of the trip.

With the theory laid out, let's see if I live up to my ambitions...<br>
After the journey through Sweden I got some valuable insights on what to bring and what not to, here's what makes the cut for this adventure:

## The bike
Trek Checkpoint ALR 5, along with lights, bikelock and a ringing bell that I use to scare people. Two water bottle holders.

## The bags

Four bags in total: a frame bag, a handlebar roll, a saddle bag, and a small snack bag (the snack bag is perfect for stashing focaccia or cannoli!). Yes, I will live for over a month on my bike and three bags. This is just an overly complicated way to simulate being homeless I guess :)

## Clothing

Two pairs of bibs, three jerseys, three sets of underwear, one light rain jacket. A packable bike poncho made specially for wearing over myself and the bike. In Italy in May there is no need for full winter kit, but I can layer-over-layer to get some pretty effective warming. Helmet and glasses to protect my head. I'll also bring some casual clothes so I can eat at restaurant with some dignity.

## Tools & spares

Bicycle punp, tyre levers, patch kit, a multitool, zip ties, chain wax, and a spare link if the chain breaks. If something bigger breaks, I'll find a bike shop. Italy has bike shops. Lots of them.

## Cooking & Sleeping

Tent with all its amendments, sleeping bag, inflatable mattress and pillow, I haven't decided yet if I'll bring the cooking kit or if I'm going to buy food each day. Since the cooking kit takes much space it is much a question of economic budget against space budget... If I find it hard to fit the cooking kit I will probably do fine by eating at restaurants and buying snacks at supermarkets. Italian food has high utility, and even though I could save money by cooking my own food it takes time and mindpower to make decisions and find a good spot to cook each day, and buy ingredients. I've realised that utility goes hand in hand with convenience. Too many things to think about (where can I cook, what do I cook, where can I do the dishes, where can I find water...) may cause <em>decision fatigue</em>. Since I love italian food I might aswell leave the cooking kit at home and experience the mediterranean cuisine for real! Regardless, decision fatigue may persist... Everyone knows picking from a restaurant menu can be a seriously tough decision!

## Electronics and miscellaneous

Phone, watch, power bank, earphones, chargers. Passport, wallet and EU healthcate insurance card. Some first-aid kit stuff, flashlight and flint and steel just in case. Hiking pants as a middle ground solution to semi-casual pants and warm pants. Sunscreen, after sun, hand moisturiser, toiletries (shaving products, soap, toothbrush...). Microfiber towel, disinfection, light gloves, plastic bags, zip ties, pencil.

## Unusual items
Putting my rational self away, I also give some room for items my creative, emotional self would like to bring regardless of the cost:<br>
A block of blank pages: Wherein I can write a diary, put memoirs, or draw paintings.<br>
12 colouring pens: I bring these in case I get the feeling to create a strong memory of some location by drawing it. I want to give room to my creative side in this adventure. I haven't drawn anything serious for years, but I feel an urge to try doing it, I'm curious on learning by doing.<br>
Earplugs and blindfold: I feel in love with birdsong and sunlight, but not when trying to sleep. Earplugs and blindfold was a gamechanger in Sweden and Norway with the midnight sun. In fact, my blindfold is a textile reusable face mask. Multifunctional again :)<br>
Spork, and a small salt and pepper container: Regardless of whether I bring the cooking kit or not, a spork will come in handy. Salt to replenish the electrolytes and black pepper because it tastes amazing with anything.<br>
A compact package of high energy food: In case I get an energy dip or miscalculate the distance to the next supermarket it could be tremendously helpful to have an energy reserve.<br>
A harmonica(?): I have been thinking about buying one for a while, it feels like a perfect instrument for bikepacking. Playing some blue chords at the nightfall while washing off the impressions of a long day. Might be aquiring one on the fly. We'll see what the future brings! <br>

## The overarching rule

If it doesn't earn its weight and space, it doesn't come. The focaccia does not count against this rule.
